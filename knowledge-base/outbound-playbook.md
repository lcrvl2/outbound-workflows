# B2B Sales Playbook

Proven B2B sales frameworks, methodologies, and best practices for cold outreach, objection handling, discovery calls, pipeline management, and proposal creation.

---

## 1. Cold Email & Outreach Principles

### Email Structure

**Effective cold emails follow this structure:**

1. **First line (the hook)** - Signal-based, specific to them, under 15 words
2. **Pain hypothesis** - What they're likely struggling with based on role/signals
3. **Tease** - Brief mention of how similar companies solved this (don't pitch yet)
4. **CTA** - Low-friction ask (NOT "book a call" on first touch)

**Length rules:**
- First email: Under 100 words
- Follow-ups: 50-75 words
- Each touch must stand alone (assume they didn't read previous)

### First Line Rules

**What works:**
- Reference a specific signal (job change, funding, hiring, content they posted)
- Connect naturally to a pain point
- Under 15-20 words

**What doesn't work:**
- "I saw that you..." (overused, feels templated)
- "Love what you're building!" (hollow compliment)
- Questions as openers (feel salesy)
- Generic compliments or observations anyone could make

**Signal-based first line examples:**

| Signal | Example First Line |
|--------|-------------------|
| Hiring 5+ SDRs | "8 SDR roles in 30 days—is productivity per rep holding as you scale past 50?" |
| New VP Sales hire | "New VP + aggressive AE hiring usually means pressure to build a repeatable motion fast." |
| Recent funding | "Post-Series B is usually when outbound infrastructure becomes a bottleneck." |
| CRM migration | "Mid-migration is the best time to rebuild outbound from scratch instead of lifting broken processes." |
| Posted about scaling challenges | "Scaling challenges typically mean the current playbook isn't keeping pace with headcount." |

### Follow-Up Sequence Structure

**5-7 touch sequence framework:**

| Touch | Timing | Purpose | Angle |
|-------|--------|---------|-------|
| 1 | Day 1 | Cold open | Pattern interrupt + pain hypothesis |
| 2 | Day 3-4 | Bump | Quick bump + new value angle |
| 3 | Day 7 | Value add | Share insight, resource, or data point |
| 4 | Day 12 | Social proof | Brief case study or result |
| 5 | Day 18 | Pattern interrupt | Different format, curiosity hook |
| 6 | Day 25 | Breakup | Permission-based close-out |

**Rules:**
- Never say "just following up" or "circling back"
- Each touch adds new value or angle
- Vary length: short, medium, short, short, medium, short
- Subject lines: Use "Re:" sparingly, try new subjects for pattern interrupt

### What NOT to Do

- Don't send walls of text
- Don't list features
- Don't use generic AI phrases ("streamline," "leverage," "synergize," "revolutionize")
- Don't mention pricing in cold outreach
- Don't ask "is this a priority?" (signals desperation)
- Don't CC multiple people on first touch
- Don't use tracking pixels on domains you haven't warmed

## 2. Signal-Based Prospecting

### The Core Principle

Generic outreach fails because there's no reason for the prospect to respond NOW. Signal-based outreach targets people showing buying signals—they have a reason to engage.

- **Single signal** = low conversion (1-3% reply rate)
- **Multiple signals stacked** = high conversion (10-25% reply rate)

### High-Converting Signal Types

**1. Hiring Signals (38% correlation with close)**
- Posting SDR/AE roles = scaling outbound, potential pain with process
- RevOps roles = tech stack pain, looking for solutions
- Marketing roles = content/demand gen pain
- "What roles they're hiring tells you what problems they're solving"

**2. Leadership Changes (34% correlation with close)**
- New VP/CRO = 90-day window to prove themselves, open to new vendors
- New CMO = auditing tech stack, looking for quick wins
- "New leaders want quick wins—be part of their first 90-day story"

**3. Funding Events (31% correlation with close)**
- Series A-C = capital to deploy, infrastructure investment
- Window: Week 1-4 (hot), Week 5-12 (warm), 12+ weeks (cold)
- "Fresh capital means spending mode—but that window closes fast"

**4. Technology Stack Changes (29% correlation with close)**
- CRM migration = open to rebuilding processes
- New tool adoption = indicates budget and willingness to invest
- Competitor uninstall = unhappy, looking for alternatives

**5. Content/Engagement Signals**
- Liked/commented on relevant content = aware, interested
- Posted about challenges = self-identified pain
- Asked questions in communities = actively seeking solutions

**6. Website Visitor Intent**
- Visited pricing page 3x = high intent
- Viewed case studies = evaluating solutions
- Downloaded content = research mode

### Signal Stacking (The Multiplier Effect)

Layer signals for maximum conversion:

| Signal Combination | Expected Reply Rate |
|-------------------|-------------------|
| Single signal | 2-3% |
| 2 signals | 8-10% |
| 3 signals | 12-15% |
| 4+ signals | 20-25% |

**Example stack:**
- New VP Sales (signal 1)
- Hiring 5 SDRs (signal 2)
- Just adopted HubSpot (signal 3)
- Posted about "scaling challenges" (signal 4)

This prospect is 10x more likely to respond than a generic ICP-fit company.

## 3. Discovery Call Methodology

### SPICED Framework

Use SPICED to prepare assumptions based on research—don't use it as an interrogation script.

| Letter | Element | What to Uncover |
|--------|---------|-----------------|
| S | Situation | Their current state, what's happening now |
| P | Pain | What's broken, what's costing them |
| I | Impact | Business consequences of inaction |
| C | Critical Event | Why now matters, timing pressure |
| E | Decision Criteria | What they need to see to decide |
| D | Decision Process | Who's involved, how they buy |

### The Key Mindset Shift

**Don't ask what you should already know. Make an assumption. Let them correct you.**

❌ Old approach: "Tell me about your business and what brought you to this call?"

✅ New approach: "I saw you posted 5 AE roles and brought on a new VP Sales—I'm guessing she's under pressure to build a repeatable motion before those reps start. Close?"

**Why this works:**
- Shows you did homework
- Gives them permission to correct you (builds trust)
- Forces them to reveal actual situation
- Positions you as advisor, not vendor

### Discovery Questions by Category

**Pain Questions:**
- "What's the cost of this problem staying unsolved for another 6 months?"
- "How long has this been an issue?"
- "What have you tried before?"
- "Why didn't that work?"

**Timeline/Urgency Questions:**
- "What happens if this doesn't get solved by [quarter end]?"
- "Is there a specific event driving the timeline?"
- "What's the cost of waiting?"

**Process Questions:**
- "Walk me through how you're handling this today, step by step."
- "Where does the process break down?"
- "Who else touches this workflow?"

**Stakeholder Questions:**
- "Who else needs to be involved in this decision?"
- "Who would block this if they weren't aligned?"
- "When you've bought solutions like this before, what did the process look like?"

**Budget Questions:**
- "How do you typically evaluate ROI on investments like this?"
- "Is there budget allocated for this, or is this a new initiative?"
- "What would success need to look like to justify the investment?"

### The First 5 Minutes

**Minute 1: The Hook**
Start with your most compelling signal observation.

Format: "Before we dive in, I noticed [SPECIFIC OBSERVATION]. I'm curious—[STRATEGIC QUESTION]?"

**Minutes 2-3: Pattern Recognition**
Reference 2-3 adjacent signals to show depth.

"That tracks with a few other things I saw—[signal 2], [signal 3]. Usually when we see this pattern, it means [hypothesis]."

**Minutes 4-5: Transition to Confirmation**
Bridge from research to their reality.

"I could be totally wrong here, but based on what I'm seeing, I'd guess [pain hypothesis]. Does that resonate, or am I off base?"

### What to Avoid

- Don't treat discovery like an interview
- Don't ask 20+ questions (interrogation vs. conversation)
- Don't ask "does that make sense?" (conversation killer)
- Don't forget to set a clear next step before ending

## 4. Objection Handling

### Core Philosophy

Objections aren't rejections—they're requests for more information or reassurance. The goal is to understand the real concern behind the stated objection.

### "Too Expensive" Framework

1. **Acknowledge** (don't dismiss): "I hear you—it's a significant investment."
2. **Ask what they're comparing to** (anchor the conversation): "Help me understand—what are you comparing this to?"
3. **Reframe as investment** (focus on cost of inaction): "What's the cost of not solving this for another 6 months?"
4. **Use specific ROI language**: "Companies similar to yours typically see [specific result] within [timeframe]."
5. **End with forward momentum**: "If the ROI made sense, what would the path forward look like?"

**Key insight:** Price objections are often really about unclear value, not actual budget constraints.

### Competitor Displacement Framework

1. **Acknowledge what competitor does well** (shows you're informed, not defensive)
2. **Ask about their experience**: "What's working? What's not?"
3. **Identify gaps without direct criticism**: "Interesting—how are they handling [known weakness]?"
4. **Position your unique value against those gaps**
5. **Use proof points**: "Companies who switched from [competitor] typically mention [specific improvement]."

**Never bash competitors.** It makes you look insecure. Let the prospect discover the gaps.

### "Not the Right Time" Framework

1. **Validate their timeline concern**: "Totally get it—timing matters."
2. **Quantify the cost of waiting**: "What does another quarter of [problem] cost in terms of [lost revenue/efficiency/competitive disadvantage]?"
3. **Offer low-commitment next step**: "What if we did a [pilot/audit/limited scope] to validate this works before you commit fully?"
4. **Create soft deadline tied to their goals**: "You mentioned wanting to hit [goal] by [date]—if we started in [month], we'd be able to [outcome]."

### "Need to Think About It" Framework

**This objection usually means there's an unspoken concern.** Your job is to surface it.

1. **Normalize it**: "Of course—this is a big decision."
2. **Diagnose the real blocker**: "When you say 'think about it,' is it more about [timing], [budget], [stakeholder alignment], or something else?"
3. **Address the real concern** once surfaced
4. **Set concrete next step**: "Would it help if I sent you [specific resource] to share with your team? Then we could reconnect on [specific date] to discuss their questions."

### Common Objection Responses

| Objection | Response Angle |
|-----------|---------------|
| "Send me more info" | "Happy to—what specifically would be most useful? I don't want to send a generic deck." |
| "We're good for now" | "Totally fair. Out of curiosity, what would need to change for this to become a priority?" |
| "We use [competitor]" | "Nice—they're solid at [X]. How's it working for [their specific use case]?" |
| "Talk to my team first" | "Of course. Would it help if I joined that conversation to answer technical questions directly?" |
| "Budget's already allocated" | "When does your planning cycle happen? I'd love to be part of next quarter's conversation." |

## 5. Pipeline Management

### Deal Risk Signals

Watch for these red flags:

| Risk Signal | What It Means | Action |
|------------|---------------|--------|
| No next step scheduled | Deal likely to stall | Never end a call without locking the next meeting |
| Champion gone quiet (7+ days) | Priorities shifted or internal blocker | Send value-add, not "checking in" |
| No access to economic buyer | Can't close without them | Multi-thread immediately |
| Timeline keeps slipping | Low priority or competing initiative | Requantify pain and urgency |
| Competitor mentioned without resolution | Active evaluation | Address head-on with displacement framework |
| Vague responses to budget questions | Not a real evaluation | Qualify harder or disqualify |

### Stage-Based Next Steps

| Stage | Goal | Recommended Next Step |
|-------|------|----------------------|
| Discovery | Confirm pain + multi-thread | Schedule call with additional stakeholder |
| Demo | Technical validation | Get their technical team's feedback |
| Proposal | Create urgency + remove blockers | Set decision timeline with mutual action plan |
| Negotiation | Close | Final alignment call with all decision makers |

### Re-Engagement Sequences (Champion Gone Quiet)

**Touch 1 (Day 7):** Add new value
- "Saw this [article/data point/case study] and thought of our conversation about [specific topic]."
- NOT: "Just checking in"

**Touch 2 (Day 14):** Reference their world
- "Noticed [company news/job post/content they shared]. How's that impacting [related initiative]?"

**Touch 3 (Day 21):** Permission-based close
- "I want to be respectful of your time. Should I assume this isn't a priority right now, or did something shift internally?"

The "breakup" email often gets responses because it gives them permission to say no—which paradoxically makes them more likely to re-engage.

## 6. Proposal & Pricing Strategy

### Pricing Structure Decision Framework

| Factor | Monthly Favored | Annual Favored |
|--------|-----------------|----------------|
| Urgency | Low urgency, needs flexibility | High urgency, committed |
| Budget certainty | Budget unclear | Budget confirmed |
| Trust level | New relationship | Established trust |
| Deal size | Smaller deals | Larger deals (discount incentive) |

| Factor | Tiered Favored | Flat Favored |
|--------|----------------|--------------|
| Scope complexity | Multiple deliverables | Single deliverable |
| Upsell potential | Clear expansion path | Standalone engagement |
| Buyer type | Sophisticated buyers | Prefer simplicity |

### Proposal Structure That Wins

1. **Executive Summary** (their pain, your solution, expected outcome)
   - Write this FOR them to forward internally
   - Include their specific numbers/goals

2. **Scope of Work** (clear deliverables, no ambiguity)
   - Be explicit about what's included AND excluded
   - Use their language, not yours

3. **Timeline** (phases, milestones, dependencies)
   - Call out what you need from them
   - Build in buffer for their delays

4. **Investment** (pricing + what's included)
   - Anchor high with full value before showing price
   - Include payment options if appropriate

5. **Why Us** (proof, differentiators, team)
   - Relevant case studies (similar company, similar challenge)
   - Name the team members who will work on their account

6. **Next Steps** (specific action required)
   - Make it easy to say yes
   - Include clear deadline if appropriate

### Scope Creep Prevention

**Red flags that predict scope creep:**
- "Oh, can you also just..." during scoping
- Vague deliverables in their brief
- No clear success metrics defined
- Multiple stakeholders with different expectations
- Rush to close without proper scoping

**Prevention tactics:**
- Document EVERYTHING in writing
- Include explicit "out of scope" section in proposals
- Build in change request process upfront
- Price in buffer for expected scope adjustments
- Get sign-off on scope before starting work

## 7. Reply Classification & Prioritization

### Reply Categories

| Classification | Signals | Response Timing |
|---------------|---------|-----------------|
| **Hot** | Clear interest, asked specific question, wants to talk | Within 1 hour |
| **Warm** | Curious, asked for more info, open but not urgent | Within 4 hours |
| **Objection** | Interested but has specific concern | Within 2 hours |
| **Not Now** | Timing issue, follow up later | Add to nurture, follow up in 30-90 days |
| **Not Interested** | Clear no, unsubscribe request | Log and close, respect their decision |
| **Referral** | Not them, but pointing elsewhere | Thank them, pursue the referral |

### Response Principles

- **Match their energy**: Short reply = short response
- **Answer their actual question**: Don't deflect to a pitch
- **One CTA per response**: Don't overwhelm with options
- **Never be defensive**: Even with objections, stay curious and helpful

## 8. Email Copy Rules

### Words/Phrases to Avoid

**Generic AI phrases:**
- "I hope this email finds you well"
- "I wanted to reach out"
- "Streamline," "leverage," "synergize," "revolutionize"
- "Touch base," "circle back"
- "Quick question" (not quick, and it's a pitch)

**Desperate signals:**
- "Just checking in"
- "Following up on my last email"
- "Did you get a chance to..."
- "When would be a good time..."

**Salesy language:**
- Feature lists
- "We help companies like yours..."
- "Our solution..."
- Industry jargon they don't use

### Words/Phrases That Work

**Opening lines:**
- Direct observations: "Noticed," "Saw," "Looks like"
- Curiosity hooks: "Quick hypothesis," "Curious if"
- Pattern recognition: "Usually when X happens, Y follows"

**Body copy:**
- Specific numbers over vague claims
- "Companies like [specific name]" over "companies like yours"
- Questions over statements
- Short sentences, active voice

**CTAs:**
- "Worth a conversation?"
- "Open to exploring this?"
- "Should I send over [specific thing]?"
- "Would it help if I [specific action]?"

## 9. Meeting & Calendar Hygiene

### Setting Next Steps

**Always secure the next step before ending any call:**

1. **Time-frame it**: "Does Tuesday or Wednesday work better?"
2. **Make it outcome-driven**: Not "follow-up call" but "pricing review with CFO"
3. **Confirm attendees**: "Who else should we loop in?"
4. **Send invite live**: "I'll send the invite now while we're both here"

### No-Show Prevention

- Send calendar invite immediately after booking
- Send reminder email 24 hours before
- Send reminder email/text 1 hour before
- Include clear agenda in the invite
- For high-value meetings: have backup contact method

### Post-Call Follow-Up

Send within 2 hours of every call:
1. Recap what was discussed (2-3 bullets)
2. Confirm agreed next steps
3. Attach any promised resources
4. Reconfirm next meeting date/time

## Key Principles

- Research before reaching out
- Signal-based timing beats volume
- Assumptions are better than interrogations
- Value-add beats check-ins
- Specific beats generic
- Always set next steps
